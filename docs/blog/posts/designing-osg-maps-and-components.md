# Designing OSG Maps and Components

In OSG, the map is not decoration and not a neutral overview of the world. It is a design instrument that determines what players can point to, contest, and use as leverage. The handbook and system reference both make the same core claim: what is on the map exists as arguable game space, and what is not on the map is usually only background, too vague to support strong action claims.

That is why maps matter more than they first appear to. A good map does not show everything. It shows the parts of the world the game is actually about, and it does so clearly enough that players can immediately see where pressure, movement, and conflict are likely to happen.

## The map defines scope

The handbook puts this very sharply: the map determines the scope of argument. If players can see a city, a pass, a route, or a port, they will use it in actions, negotiations, and reports. If they cannot see it, it has far less practical weight even if it “exists” in setting lore. The system reference echoes this by calling the map the game’s argued playing field and placing it after briefs in the design workflow, as the stage where faction stakes gain a visible surface.

This is an important corrective because many scenario maps try to be comprehensive. They behave like atlas pages rather than play tools. OSG map design works in the opposite direction. It begins by asking what players should be arguing about, and then includes only the locations and relationships needed to make those arguments concrete.

That is why the texts insist the map is an editorial statement, not a neutral representation. A map with three cities and two routes is not incomplete if those are the only strategically relevant locations. It is doing its job.

## Geographic or schematic

Both sources distinguish two basic map types: geographic and schematic. A geographic map represents physical space, territory, distance, terrain, chokepoints, and routes. A schematic map represents relationships, dependencies, institutions, or networks more than literal distance.

The choice between them is not cosmetic. It shapes the kinds of arguments the game naturally produces. If armies move, ports matter, and supply routes can be blocked, a geographic map supports the fiction directly. If the core conflict is about institutional recognition, trade dependency, communications, or political influence rather than physical maneuver, a schematic map may work better.

The handbook’s Corentine example is explicitly “primarily geographic,” because factions occupy meaningful positions on a peninsula, Caldrath’s army has to move through or around Essaveth, and control of Corenth matters as a political center. But even there, the map is not a topographic simulation. It is a schematic of power-relevant distances. That formulation is useful well beyond the example. The goal is not realism for its own sake. The goal is readable leverage.

## What belongs on the map

The handbook identifies three categories that belong on a map: contested zones, faction starting positions, and named locations that are arguable. The system reference compresses this into essentially the same checklist, saying the map should include contested zones, asymmetrically placed starting positions, and named locations that will matter in actions and reports.

Contested zones are crucial because they are where faction interests collide. Without them, factions tend to develop in parallel rather than in contact. Starting positions matter because where a faction begins relative to the crisis and to the contested zones is itself a form of leverage or weakness. Named arguable locations matter because players can only point at what the game has made legible.

The negative rule matters just as much: do not put everything on the map. Unknown regions, irrelevant settlements, decorative landmarks, and generic empty space create clutter rather than depth. In OSG, excess detail often weakens play by obscuring the locations that actually matter.

## Minimum viable map

One of the most useful practical claims in Chapter 10 is that a very small map is often enough. The handbook says a map with three to five locations and three to five connection lines can be sufficient to run a game. The system reference gives the same range in its general map guidance. That is a valuable permission structure for designers who assume a proper scenario needs a large or beautiful map.

The point is readability. A minimum viable map should be understandable at a glance by someone who has never seen it before. If it requires a lecture before anyone can use it, it is probably too complex for the function it is meant to serve.

This is also why the handbook cites small military matrix-game maps as valid precedents, including a Falklands example built from only a handful of named locations and simple connection lines. The lesson is not to imitate that exact structure, but to understand that a map can be strategically complete without being visually rich.

## Components should move

The handbook is just as clear about components as it is about the map itself. The core principle is that components should be moveable. The system reference repeats this in shorter form by defining minimum components as faction tokens, contested-zone markers, and optional named asset markers.

This matters because change in OSG should become visible. A static list of territorial control is informational, but it is less playable than a token being placed, shifted, or removed from the table. When a marker moves off a location, everyone sees that the fictional state has changed. The map becomes a live board of consequences rather than a passive illustration.

The minimum component set is intentionally simple. One token per faction, markers for control or contestation, and perhaps a few named assets if those assets are established and likely to matter. The handbook explicitly says these do not need to be elaborate. Index cards, poker chips, and sticky notes are enough if they are legible and updateable.

## The map should match leverage

A good OSG map does not only show space. It shows the kinds of leverage the factions actually possess. The handbook says map design should be matched to faction leverage types, and the system reference says the same in nearly identical language. This is one of the most important design links in the whole workflow.

If you have factions built around pass control, coastal access, route denial, and military movement, geography needs to be visible. If you have factions built around recognition chains, institutional authority, or information networks, a schematic map of dependencies may do more work than a physical one. A mismatch between leverage and map type creates weak arguments because the game surface does not support the faction design.

The Corentine example demonstrates this well. Caldrath’s leverage involves army movement from the north, Essaveth’s leverage involves the passes, Saivorn’s leverage involves ports and sea access, and Corenth matters because presence there affects political settlement. Those faction identities become easier to understand because the map makes their positions visible. The map is not separate from the roster. It clarifies the roster.

## Good maps exclude aggressively

One of the most counterintuitive lessons in Chapter 10 is that exclusion is a strength. The handbook explicitly says that details like interior geography, every road, every minor noble seat, or every garrison location can simply be omitted when they are not central to the problem. The Corentine worked example says those details “don’t exist in the game” because the map has correctly excluded them.

This is an important design stance. A map is not weak because it refuses to answer every imaginable question. It is strong when it names the few locations that will matter repeatedly and leaves the rest outside the frame. That keeps action arguments focused and adjudication consistent.

The system reference supports the same idea indirectly by insisting that named locations on the map should be locations that will actually appear in actions and reports. In other words, include what will be argued, not what would exist in a complete encyclopedia of the setting.

## The map prevents abstraction drift

The handbook’s workflow chapter warns that skipping the map often causes arguments to become abstract and unresolved, especially around geography. The system reference makes the same point by strongly recommending a map even though Problem, Actors, and Briefs are technically enough to run a game. That distinction is useful. A map is not strictly mandatory, but in practice it prevents a lot of avoidable friction.

Without a map, players may still imagine territory, routes, centers of influence, and adjacency, but each player may imagine them differently. That forces the referee to settle questions on the fly that could have been made legible before play began. The map is therefore not just a visual aid. It is a shared assumptions tool.

This links directly to the rest of the OSG design philosophy. Problems create contested space, factions create distinct responses, briefs create individual stakes, and the map gives all of that a visible arena. When that arena is missing or unclear, the whole system has less to push against.

## A practical map checklist

A map is probably ready when you can answer yes to most of these.

- Does it show contested zones that multiple factions have reason to care about ?
- Are faction starting positions visually distinct and asymmetrical ?
- Are all locations likely to appear in actions and reports named on the map ?
- Is the map readable at a glance without explanation ?
- Does the map type, geographic or schematic, match the leverage types of the factions ?
- Are components moveable rather than static, with at least one token per faction and markers for control or contestation ?

If not, the map may still be attractive, but it is probably not yet doing full design work.

## Why maps are part of scenario design, not presentation

The system reference places the map as Step 4 in scenario design, after problem, actors, and briefs. The handbook’s workflow does the same and treats the map as the stage where arguments become spatially or structurally legible. That placement matters because it shows the map is not a finishing touch added after the real design is done.

In OSG, the map is part of how the scenario thinks. It decides what can be contested, what counts as adjacency, what can be blocked, what can be pointed at, and what remains background. A strong map therefore does not merely illustrate the game. It helps produce the game.
