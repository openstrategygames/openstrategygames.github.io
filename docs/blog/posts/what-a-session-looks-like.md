# What an OSG Session Actually Looks Like

People can understand a form in the abstract and still not know what it feels like in motion. That is especially true of OSG, because its identity is not located in a single mechanic but in a repeating sequence: brief, action, adjudication, report, and debrief. The handbook, system reference, and guiding principles all describe that sequence in compatible terms, with a shared crisis, faction briefs, one private action per turn, referee adjudication, public reporting, a fixed endpoint, and a closing talk-back as the basic rhythm of play.

So this post is not about what OSG is in theory. It is about what happens when people actually sit down to play. If you want the shortest possible version, it goes like this: you receive a faction brief, you study a shared problem, you negotiate freely, you submit one action in private, the referee adjudicates all actions, a public report reveals the changed world, and after a fixed number of turns everyone debriefs and self-assesses what they achieved.

## Before Turn 1

An OSG session begins before the first action is written. Players are given the public rules, the broad outline of the world, the initial problem, and predetermined objectives and assets through briefings, while each faction also receives its own private position and objectives. In the system reference, the general brief includes the problem, shared world context, public descriptions of the factions, the turn structure, and the expectations formula, while each private brief adds that faction’s paired objectives and especially significant position details.

This opening matters because OSG does not ask players to discover who they are through play in the way many character-centered games do. It asks them to begin from a strategic role with defined interests, leverage, and constraints, then decide how to pursue those interests under pressure. The handbook describes the first-turn experience as cautious and exploratory: players probe, establish footholds, signal intent, and test possible allies before they fully understand how everyone else is approaching the crisis.

Many games also include a Turn Zero before formal play begins. Turn Zero is a structured clarification phase in which players ask questions about world assumptions, edge cases in the starting situation, and procedural rules, with the referee publishing answers so the group starts from a shared understanding rather than discovering basic misunderstandings mid-game. That step is not the dramatic heart of play, but it prevents later confusion from consuming early turns.

## The turn

Once the game begins, the fundamental unit of play is the turn. The sources present the turn sequence very plainly: the referee announces a deadline, players submit actions privately, the referee adjudicates them, a public report is published, and then a new submission window opens for the next turn. The whole form lives inside that loop.

Each player controls a faction and submits exactly one action per turn, not two things bundled together. That one action has a three-part structure: what the faction is doing, what outcome it wants, and what leverage makes that outcome plausible given the established fiction. The handbook and system reference both stress that these parts do different work: action commits the player to a concrete move, outcome makes intent explicit before adjudication, and leverage forces the player to ground the plan in resources, relationships, position, or prior events rather than vague assertion.

At the same time, talking is free. Players can negotiate, threaten, coordinate, share intelligence, and bluff without spending their turn, because only enacted strategic conduct costs the single action. That distinction is one reason OSG produces so much political texture between reports: the social field is active almost constantly, but decisive moves still have to be prioritized into one formal submission.

The action is sent privately to the referee rather than posted publicly. The handbook and system reference both say this prevents reactive play from dominating, allows the referee to adjudicate a whole turn before outcomes become public, and protects genuinely covert operations until the report reveals as much as should be known. In other words, the game spends part of each turn in darkness: everyone has acted, but nobody yet knows what the world now looks like.

## Adjudication and report

After submissions close, the referee takes over. The referee is not presented as a story-authoring game master but as the person who adjudicates actions, writes reports, and preserves internal consistency in the fiction under a model the texts call Benevolent Dictatorship: full authority, no public argument before the ruling. That authority exists to keep the game moving and to stop the loudest players from winning outcomes through public debate rather than through position and leverage inside the fiction.

When an action faces opposition, the referee makes a qualitative judgment about leverage. The core question is whether, given what has been established in the fiction, the acting faction has enough advantage that success is more likely than not; if yes, leverage is Strong, and if not, it is Weak. The standard mechanic then rolls 2d6, keeping the higher die for Strong leverage and the lower die for Weak leverage, with the kept die determining whether the desired outcome happens, happens imperfectly, or fails with possible extra consequences at the extremes. When an action is unopposed, the desired outcome occurs, but the referee still decides how that success lands in the world and what new complications or openings it creates.

The important thing is that adjudication does not end with “yes” or “no.” The handbook repeatedly insists that every outcome, successful or not, leaves a mark on the world, and the system reference frames this as replacing hard blocks with friction: not “you cannot do this,” but “you can do this, and here is the cost, consequence, or opening it creates”. Referees are also told to test outcomes against RAT: are they Reasonable, Actionable, and Traceable ? Those three criteria are less visible than the dice, but they are what make a turn feel coherent when the report arrives.

Then comes the public report, which the handbook calls the heartbeat of the game. A report does two jobs at once: it records what happened and re-seeds the game by placing new hooks, threats, opportunities, and uncertainties in front of the factions. The recommended style is brief, factual, and third-person, more like a news roundup than a dramatic scene, with the world described as it now stands rather than the referee explaining the hidden logic of the ruling.

This report phase is the emotional pivot of the turn. The handbook describes the player experience very clearly: you act, you wait in uncertainty, and then the report reveals several developments you did not predict, one of which may demand immediate response while another quietly threatens your long-term position. That is why the report is not just bookkeeping. It is the moment the game becomes public again.

A compact notation example from *Factionlog* shows the same sequence in miniature: action, leverage grade, roll, adjudicated result, board-state change, then a short public summary of what everyone now knows. That document is about logging rather than theory, but it is useful because it demonstrates how stable the actual-play anatomy is across explanation and recordkeeping: the game keeps moving from intention, to judgment, to changed world, to renewed pressure.

## Endgame and debrief

After a fixed number of turns, the session ends. That endpoint is part of the structure from the beginning, not an afterthought, and both the guiding principles and the later documents tie self-assessment directly to that fixed ending. Players do not keep playing until one side is mechanically eliminated or until a campaign naturally loses steam; instead, they reach a defined stop and then ask what their faction actually accomplished within that span.

Before the debrief, the referee publishes a closing frame, either as a state-of-world table or a short narrative epilogue, so the table has a common picture of the final situation before stepping out of role. The recommended debrief sequence is consistent across the texts: a brief in-role farewell, uninterrupted self-assessment reveals, open table response, out-of-role reflection, and finally referee reflection on what the scenario design produced or failed to produce. The referee does not issue official verdicts on whether players achieved their objectives; disagreement is allowed to stand and becomes part of the post-game conversation.

This is one of the points where OSG looks most unlike a lot of adjacent play forms. The debrief is not optional cleanup but a formal part of the experience, and the handbook is explicit that without it, self-assessment stays private and the game loses one of its richest meaning-making stages. The post-game conversation is where players reveal hidden priorities, explain why strategies changed, contest one another’s claims, and expose what the scenario design actually encouraged in practice.

## Why this sequence matters

Looking at the sequence as a whole helps explain why OSG feels distinctive in practice. You do not take turns acting in full public view, as in many tactical games, and you do not inhabit a single protagonist moving through a referee-authored situation, as in many RPGs; instead, you occupy a strategic role, commit one move in private, and wait for the shared world to come back changed by everyone’s decisions at once. The rhythm is therefore cyclical rather than scene-based: brief, action, silence, revelation, repositioning, and eventual retrospective judgment.

That is also why descriptions of OSG that focus only on the dice, or only on referee adjudication, tend to feel incomplete. The real unit of experience is not the roll but the whole loop that carries a faction from its brief, through pressured choice, into public consequence, and finally into post-game interpretation. If you want to know what OSG actually looks like at the table, that loop is the answer.
