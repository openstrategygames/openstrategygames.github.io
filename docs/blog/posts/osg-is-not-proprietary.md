# Open Strategy Game Is Not a Proprietary Label

When people first encounter the term **Open Strategy Game**, one of the most predictable misunderstandings is also one of the most revealing: they assume it must be a product name, a publisher label, or a house system owned by a particular designer. That reaction is understandable. Tabletop culture is full of named games, branded engines, and tightly identified creator-led movements. But OSG is not that kind of term. It was adopted to describe a recognizable form of play that multiple designers were already making, and one of the explicit reasons for the term was that it removed the form from individual ownership rather than attaching it more firmly to one person, line, or company.

That matters because the way we name things shapes how we think about them. If a term sounds like a product, people look for the core book, the official edition, the trademark holder, and the definitive rules text. If a term names a form instead, people ask a different set of questions: what makes this recognizable, what are its recurring structures, what can vary, and what kind of play does it enable. OSG belongs in that second category.

## A term for a form

The handbook’s account of the term’s origin is direct. In 2021, Chris McDowall and Sam Doebler were running very similar kinds of games under different labels, and the move toward “open strategy games” was not presented as a marketing refresh but as recognition that something distinct had emerged and needed a name that communicated what it was without requiring constant explanation. The guiding-principles text says much the same thing in plainer language: McDowall and Doebler discussed using the term to avoid confusion around “Matrix Game,” to avoid the burden of explaining the word “matrix,” and to remove the form from individual ownership.

Those details are important because they tell us what the name was for. It was not meant to designate a single official game line. It was not meant to turn one designer’s implementation into the standard that all others must license, imitate, or defer to. It was meant to name a playable form clearly enough that people could recognize related work when they saw it.

This is one reason the handbook repeatedly talks about OSG as a “playable design framework” rather than a rigid definition or closed rules product. The system reference does something similar by presenting a generic, setting-neutral framework that can be used as written, adapted, or built on for new scenarios and variants. That is the language of a form, not the language of a brand bible.

## Not a boxed rules engine

Calling OSG “not a product” does not mean it has no structure. On the contrary, the sources are consistent that OSG has a recognizable common structure: a shared problem, asymmetric factions, one action per actor per turn, private submission, referee adjudication, public reports, a fixed endpoint, and debrief or self-assessment at the end. But having a structure is not the same thing as being a boxed, singular, commercial object.

A product usually answers questions of identity through publication. Which book is the real one? Which edition is current? Which version is official? What content is core, and what content is third-party? OSG does not sit comfortably inside that frame. The handbook explicitly says it is treating OSG as a framework rather than a rigid definition, and the guiding-principles document goes further by saying outright that it is “NOT a definition” and that OSGs may deviate from those points. That is not how proprietary systems normally talk about themselves.

The distinction becomes clearer if you compare OSG to a branded RPG engine. A branded engine normally implies a stable package of mechanics, terminology, and official support. OSG, by contrast, is centered less on a fixed rules chassis than on a recurring play procedure and adjudication logic: you play a faction, not a single protagonist; you pursue objectives inside a shared crisis; you submit one action with an intended outcome and stated leverage; a referee adjudicates what happens; the public report changes the strategic situation; and the game ends after a set number of turns with self-assessment and debrief. That is a form with a center of gravity, but it is not a product SKU.

## Not a personal label

It is just as important to say that OSG is not a personal brand label attached to one creator’s output. The sources consistently place McDowall and Doebler in the immediate naming history, but they also place the form inside a longer lineage that includes Chris Engle’s Matrix Games and Tom Mouat’s practical development of the format in military and professional settings. That broader lineage matters because it prevents the term from collapsing into authorship.

The handbook is careful on this point. It traces the term and foundational examples to McDowall’s *Sunrise Expansion* and Doebler’s *Landover*, but then immediately says the lineage runs deeper, through Engle and Mouat, and that what survived into OSG was the core mechanic: declare an action, support it with reasons, and let a referee determine what happens. In other words, the form was inherited, adapted, stripped of some scaffolding, and clarified for entertainment play. That is a history of development, not of ownership.

This also explains why “not a brand” is more than a semantic preference. If the term were treated as a personal label, every new implementation would tend to be judged by fidelity to one designer’s style rather than by whether it successfully produces the kind of play the form is built for. But the existing texts point in the opposite direction. They present OSG as portable across settings, scales, and scenario designs, provided the core structure still generates open strategic play through faction action, adjudication, and consequences. That makes “who owns the label?” the wrong question.

## Why the misunderstanding happens

The misunderstanding is not foolish. OSG sounds like the name of a thing you could buy. It is compact, declarative, and genre-neutral, which are all qualities shared by product names. And in hobby spaces, people are trained to look for officiality. They want to know whether a term names a specific game, an SRD, a movement, a trademark, or just a loose cluster of practices.

But the texts themselves push against that instinct. The guiding principles say these are “guiding principles” and are “subject to revision, debate, etc.” rather than authoritative final wording. The handbook repeatedly separates common structure from rigid definition and emphasizes that scenarios can vary widely in genre, scale, and texture within that structure. The system reference frames itself as a generic rules framework derived from the broader design principles rather than the proprietary codification of a single owner’s game.

In other words, the ambiguity exists because OSG occupies a middle space that hobby culture does not always name well. It is more specific than a vague aesthetic tendency, but less centralized than a publisher-owned game line. It has a recognizable procedure, a clear lineage, and shared vocabulary, yet it remains open enough that no single text can plausibly exhaust it.

## What “open” does and does not mean

The word “open” can also mislead people. Some will hear it and assume OSG must be primarily a statement about licensing. Others will hear it as a promise of maximal looseness or total DIY freedom. Neither reading quite fits the texts.

In the handbook and reference material, openness refers primarily to the nature of play: players are free to attempt what makes sense within the fiction, rather than choosing from a closed menu of moves, and strategy emerges from position, action, and consequence rather than optimization against a pre-exhausted rules list. The system reference makes the same logic visible through its emphasis on Action, Outcome, and Leverage, on qualitative referee judgment, and on the principle that every outcome should leave a mark on the world rather than simply toggling a binary success state. That is openness in action space and adjudication logic. 

At the same time, the same sources are clear that OSG is not formless. One action per turn matters. Private submission matters. Referee adjudication matters. The report cycle matters. The fixed endpoint and debrief matter. So “open” does not mean “anything goes,” and it does not mean the form dissolves into pure improvisation. It means the space of attempted action is open-ended inside a structured strategic procedure. 

That distinction supports the larger point of this article. A brand often protects identity by controlling permissible use. OSG, by contrast, preserves identity through recurring structure and recognizable play outcomes, not through centralized ownership. The form stays legible because its procedure keeps producing the same kind of experience, not because one authority polices the label. 

## What follows from this

If OSG is not a product or a brand, then discussions about it should change accordingly. The first question should not be “where is the official version?” but “what is the minimal baseline that makes something recognizably OSG?”. The next should not be “is this authorized?” but “does this preserve the core form while varying optional elements?”. Those are design questions, not ownership questions. 

It also means that examples should be treated as examples, not as sealed canon. *Sunrise Expansion* matters as a foundational case in the naming history, and Doebler’s principles matter because they articulate recurrent practice, but neither role requires treating one scenario or one document as the whole form. The texts themselves do not encourage that reading. They encourage recognition, adaptation, and careful separation of core procedures from optional layers such as bonuses, NPAs, overflow roles, or specific variant mechanics.

There is also a practical benefit to resisting product thinking. Product thinking encourages consumers; form thinking encourages designers and referees. The handbook is explicitly a design framework, and the system reference is explicitly generic and adaptable. That orientation invites people to make new scenarios, test new participation structures, and argue about boundaries without waiting for an official line to settle the matter. In a form built around adjudicated, evolving situations, that is a feature rather than a flaw. 

## The boundary this article is drawing

To say OSG is not a product or brand is not to say the term is meaningless, infinitely elastic, or beyond criticism. The sources do not support that. They support a recognizable core, a shared lineage, and a stable enough set of procedures that the form can be discussed coherently. What they reject is the idea that coherence must come from proprietary ownership, singular authorship, or a definitive boxed edition. 

That is the real point. OSG names a form of play. It has ancestors, exemplars, procedures, and debates. It has influential texts and foundational implementations. But it is not a product line to be bought whole, and it is not a brand label that turns recognition into ownership. The term exists precisely so that people can point to the form itself. 

In the next post, the obvious follow-up is why that matters. If OSG is a term for a form rather than a brand, what work does the term actually do? Why not keep saying “Matrix Game,” “free Kriegsspiel-like strategy game,” or just “a referee-run faction game”? The answer is that naming is not cosmetic here. The term clarifies what this thing is for, what it inherits, and what it is trying to preserve.
