# Traits, Bonuses, and Spendable Leverage

OSG bonuses are optional, but when they are used well, they add a useful extra layer of asymmetry on top of faction identity. The handbook and system reference both define them as named, spendable, one-use assets tied to a faction’s starting position, meant to strengthen leverage in a specific action rather than function as permanent powers. That distinction is the key to designing them well.

A good bonus gives a player a sharper decision. A bad bonus makes decisions less interesting by acting like an auto-win button, a vague universal modifier, or a pile of generic extra power. Bonuses work best when they are concrete, limited, and fictionally grounded.

## Bonuses are optional on purpose

The handbook explicitly says spendable bonuses are optional and suggests that first-time referees skip them on a first run, then add them later once the core mechanics are familiar. The system reference places bonuses after problem, actors, briefs, and map in the design workflow, which makes their status clear: they are an additional layer, not part of the irreducible minimum.

That is an important framing choice. Bonuses are not there to fix a weak scenario. If the problem, factions, briefs, and map are not already generating pressure, bonuses will not solve that. At best they will add noise. At worst they will hide the more basic design problem.

So the right question is not “what cool powers can I hand out?” It is “what specific, expendable resources would meaningfully deepen the play style this faction already has?”. That is a narrower and much more productive design target.

## Bonus versus trait

The handbook draws a very clear distinction between bonuses and traits. A trait is a persistent characteristic that strengthens leverage whenever relevant, such as having the largest standing army or a durable communications network. A bonus is a resource that can be spent and then is gone, like an emergency reserve, a saved intelligence file, or a one-off treaty concession. The system reference makes the same distinction in its glossary and design summary.

This distinction matters because designers often conflate “something the faction has” with “something the faction can spend.” If a capability is central to the faction’s identity and should matter repeatedly, it belongs in the position field as a trait, not in the bonus list. If it is a specific asset whose use should require timing and sacrifice, it probably belongs as a bonus.

This is also what gives bonuses their strategic value. Traits shape the background of many actions. Bonuses create moments of commitment. Choosing to spend a bonus means choosing that this is the turn or the situation where the asset matters enough to cash in.

## Narrative names matter

The handbook says the most important design decision for a bonus is what to call it. A generic label like “Military Advantage Token x3” describes game effect but contributes almost nothing to the fiction or to leverage argument. A narrative label like “Io Dust 3x” or “The Titan Accord” does much more: it suggests context, use case, and boundaries all at once. The system reference condenses this into a clear rule: bonuses should be narratively named, not mechanically named.

This is not just a style preference. Narrative naming improves play because it helps players make stronger in-fiction claims and helps referees police scope without sounding arbitrary. If a player spends “Emergency Fuel Reserve 1x,” the intended use is already narrower and more legible than if they spend “+1 Strong Action”. The name itself carries design information.

It also makes the game more memorable. Players remember having spent “The Titan Accord” or “Martian Water Rights” far more readily than they remember spending a generic bonus token. That matters because OSG is partly about creating a credible and discussable narrative, not just resolving outcomes.

## Bonuses should strengthen leverage, not replace adjudication

The handbook’s strongest warning is against the auto-success bonus. A bonus should shift leverage from weak to strong in a specific context, not guarantee success no matter what else is true. The system reference says the same directly: a bonus should strengthen leverage, not override other factors.

This is a crucial design principle because OSG resolution depends on the referee still making a meaningful judgment about the fiction. If a bonus bypasses that judgment entirely, the system becomes flatter. The player is no longer deciding whether their leverage is strong enough in context; they are just cashing in certainty.

The handbook therefore recommends bounded scope. A bonus should apply to one action, in one kind of situation, and its narrative name or explicit description should indicate where it does and does not matter. “Martian Water Rights 1x” should help in water-access disputes, not in unrelated diplomatic or military actions on another planet. That boundedness is what keeps bonuses interesting.

## Scope and quantity shape tone

The handbook also points out that not all bonuses should feel the same in play. A multi-use bonus and a single-use bonus create different strategic moods. A resource with three uses feels like a recurring edge a player can plan around, while a single-use bonus feels like a true trump card that creates tension about timing. The system reference includes the same logic in its checklist by saying multi-use versus single-use allocations should match the strategic style intended for each faction.

This is useful because it shows bonus design is not only about fairness, but also about tempo. A faction with several modest multi-use bonuses may feel methodical and resilient. A faction with one or two powerful one-use bonuses may feel opportunistic, explosive, or fragile. Those are different play experiences, and the choice between them should be deliberate.

The key is that these structures should emerge from the fiction and from the faction’s identity, not from a desire to distribute abstract “power points”. A mercantile faction’s repeatable bonus might represent recurring access to credit or logistics, while a desperate claimant faction might have one extraordinary document, one reserve force, or one sealed bargain it can spend at a critical moment.

## Bonuses should come from the fiction

The handbook gives a very practical design prompt: when designing bonuses, ask what specific resources, relationships, or capabilities this faction has that are not already fully captured in its position field. Then name those things, decide how often they can be spent, and that gives you your list. The system reference supports the same approach by placing bonuses after briefs and map, since the earlier design steps should already have established what kinds of assets make sense.

This is a good filter because it stops bonus design from becoming arbitrary power vending. If the brief and map already imply a faction’s leverage, the bonus list should be a sharpened extension of that leverage, not a random extra subsystem. A faction defined by ports, debt, or archives should get bonuses that intensify those realities, not unrelated tricks.

That also means some factions may naturally want fewer, stronger bonuses, while others may want several smaller ones. As long as the balance is managed, asymmetry in style is fine. What matters is that each bonus feels like it belongs to that faction.

## Imbalance is easy to create

The handbook is very explicit that bonus imbalance is a real design risk. Leverage-type asymmetry is generative because it creates different strategic paths, but bonus quantity asymmetry can create unfairness if one faction simply receives more or better expendable options than everyone else. The system reference repeats this concern by including a checklist item that no faction should have more than twice the number of high-value bonuses as any other.

This is a subtle but important point. “Asymmetric” does not mean “uneven in every possible way.” A faction with more bonuses may have more chances to force strong actions early, regardless of whether its strategic position is actually more interesting. That can front-load the game and distort the scenario before the core faction dynamics have time to matter.

The handbook suggests several useful tests. Does every faction have at least one named bonus? Is any single bonus better than comparable bonuses in most contexts? Could the faction with the largest bonus pool spend them all in the first few turns and gain a major advantage without meaningful downside? If the answer to the last two is yes, the bonus structure probably needs revision. Those are good design questions because they focus on effects in play, not just on aesthetic balance.

## Bonuses should fit the brief, not crowd it

Because bonuses are optional and subordinate, they should remain legible within the brief rather than overwhelming it. The system reference’s template puts them after objectives and position as a small “special abilities” table, not as the main body of the document. The handbook’s explanation of trait versus bonus reinforces the same hierarchy: bonuses sit on top of the faction’s core identity rather than replacing it.

That hierarchy matters. If a faction would be boring without its bonuses, the faction design is probably weak. Bonuses should sharpen an already functional role, not serve as the only reason the role exists. Likewise, if the bonus list is much longer or more detailed than the objective and position sections, the brief may be sending the wrong signal about how the faction is meant to be played.

A strong brief should still tell the player who they are, what they want, and what leverage they normally possess. Bonuses then add a few moments of exceptional intensity or flexibility. They are accents, not the whole sentence.

## A practical bonus checklist

A bonus list is probably in good shape when you can answer yes to most of these.

- Is each bonus a named, spendable, fictionally grounded asset rather than a generic token?
- Does each bonus differ clearly from a permanent trait the faction should always possess?
- Does spending a bonus strengthen leverage in a specific action without guaranteeing success?
- Is the scope bounded by the name or description, so players and referee can tell when it applies?
- Does every faction have at least one bonus if bonuses are being used at all?
- Have you avoided giving any one faction a clearly dominant bonus economy?

If not, the bonuses may still be flavorful, but they are likely to create either confusion or imbalance.

## Why good bonuses feel like opportunities

The handbook’s design logic is consistent all the way through: OSG works best when it produces meaningful choices, not mechanical certainty. Bonuses fit that philosophy when they force the player to ask whether this is the right moment, the right action, and the right cost for using a scarce edge. The system reference keeps them optional for exactly that reason: they are useful only when they deepen strategic judgment rather than short-circuit it.

So the best way to think about bonuses is not as powers. They are opportunities to commit. When they are named well, scoped well, and balanced well, they add tension, memory, and asymmetry without damaging the core adjudication logic of the game.
