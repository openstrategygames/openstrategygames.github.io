# How to Write OSG Briefs

A faction can be well designed in the abstract and still fail at the table if its brief is vague, padded, or strategically inert. In OSG, the brief is the document that turns faction design into playable decision-making. The handbook and system reference both describe it as an operational document rather than background reading, with two mandatory fields, objectives and position, plus a public/private information split that tells players not just who they are, but what they can actually do and why it matters.

That is the key idea for this post. A good brief does not try to tell the whole story of a faction. It gives the player the minimum set of facts they need to act decisively, argue leverage credibly, and self-assess meaningfully at the end. Everything else is secondary.

## What a brief is for

The handbook is unusually blunt here: the brief is not background reading, it is operational, and everything in it should be something the Actor will use. The system reference frames it the same way by making the brief the place where objectives and position are specified, with public information shared to all players and private information held only by the relevant faction. This means a brief is not a lore handout in the usual sense.

That distinction matters because many badly written briefs are really setting essays with a faction label attached. They may be flavorful, but they do not tell the player where their leverage lies, what risks matter, or what kind of success to pursue. A usable brief answers those questions early and clearly.

The public/private split is part of that usability. The handbook says a brief has public and private components, whether delivered as separate documents or combined into one player-facing sheet with a shorter public summary for everyone else. The system reference provides a generic version of the same structure: the general brief sets the shared situation and turn structure, and the private brief gives each faction its own objectives and especially significant position details. The split matters because OSG thrives on asymmetry not only of power, but of knowledge.

## Two mandatory fields

Both the handbook and the system reference say every brief has two mandatory fields: objectives and position. That sounds simple, but it is a strong design constraint. If a document does not clearly tell the player what they are trying to accomplish and what they have to work with, it is not doing the job a brief is supposed to do.

Objectives answer the question “what am I trying to achieve?”. Position answers the question “what is especially true of me, relative to others, that shapes how I can pursue those aims?”. The first gives direction. The second gives leverage, limits, and texture.

This also means that many things people are tempted to include are optional at best. Ideological history, cultural notes, administrative detail, internal hierarchy, and decorative setting facts can all be interesting, but if they do not change action design or self-assessment, they are probably not brief material. They belong elsewhere, or nowhere.

## Objectives should come in pairs

The handbook’s strongest practical advice is that objectives should be paired: one short-term objective achievable within the game’s span, and one long-term objective that may only be partially realized by laying groundwork during play. The system reference repeats this directly and builds it into the standard brief template.

This pairing does important structural work. The short-term objective gives the player a reason to act immediately and prevents the first turns from becoming hesitant drift. The long-term objective makes the end-state of the world matter and encourages decisions that are strategic rather than merely opportunistic. Good paired objectives also create tension: the move that secures short-term protection may undermine the conditions for long-term success, while chasing the ideal final outcome may leave the faction exposed in the present.

The Essaveth example from the handbook captures this well. Essaveth wants immediate guarantees of territorial integrity before Caldrath moves, but also wants its borders named and protected in whatever successor treaty emerges. Those aims overlap, but they can also compete: concessions that buy fast guarantees may weaken the final settlement, while holding out for a perfect treaty may come too late. That is a good objective pair because it produces judgment, not autopilot.

The handbook also stresses that long-term objectives are not failures just because they remain incomplete. Some long-term goals are judged by whether groundwork was successfully laid, and it is worth stating that explicitly in the brief so players do not dismiss them as unreachable. The system reference says the same thing in more general terms by defining long-term goals as objectives whose full realization may lie beyond the turn count.

## Objectives should sound like someone means them

One of the best editorial tests in the whole system is the Pithy Objectives Test. The handbook says to add an exclamation mark to the objective and read it aloud. If it sounds like something a faction leader would actually say with conviction, it probably works; if it sounds like a policy document, it probably does not. The system reference includes the same test by name.

This works because bad objectives are usually not wrong in concept, only too abstract in language. “Maintain institutional continuity during transitional governance” may describe a real interest, but it is not vivid enough to guide action or create interesting self-assessment. “Ensure that no new treaty takes effect without Conclave ratification” is much better because it is specific, arguable, and visibly conflict-generating.

That difference matters in play. Players do not think in terms of broad mission statements when they submit actions. They think in terms of concrete stakes, deadlines, fears, and opportunities. Objectives should therefore feel speakable, not bureaucratic.

## Position is not an inventory

The second mandatory field is position, and the handbook is equally clear about what it is not. Position is not a list of everything the faction possesses; it is what the faction has, lacks, or faces that is especially significant relative to the other factions. The system reference says the same thing and explicitly warns against treating it as an inventory.

This is crucial because inventory-style briefs create noise rather than clarity. If every faction gets a long list of ships, clerks, supply depots, farms, social institutions, and routine assets, the important differences disappear. A good position field highlights what changes the argument space.

The handbook offers a clean inclusion test. Position bullets should cover specific assets not obvious from the public identity, specific vulnerabilities the player needs to understand, key relationships that shape options, and geographic or positional constraints that determine what can and cannot be done. The system reference gives essentially the same advice. What belongs in the brief is what a player needs in order to make and defend actions.

The Conclave example is especially strong. It holds original treaty texts with unpublished clauses, occupies an extraterritorial temple complex, and has no military while carrying debt to Meranto. Each point is narrow, specific, and consequential. Each one changes how the faction can act. That is exactly what position bullets should do.

## Public and private should create playable tension

One of the most productive design tools in a brief is the gap between what everyone knows and what only the faction knows. The handbook notes that in some designs public and private elements are separated into distinct documents, while in others they are integrated in one private brief alongside a public summary distributed to all players. The system reference includes both a general public brief and a private faction brief in its model template.

This split matters because some of the most interesting play arises when a faction’s visible role and private motive are not identical. The handbook’s Saivorn example shows this clearly. Publicly, Saivorn is the peninsula’s dominant commercial power seeking stable sea lanes and free movement; privately, the death of the Arbiter is not entirely unwelcome, because the old treaty order imposed costly concessions and Saivorn wants a new arrangement, not simply restoration. That private line changes the whole reading of the faction.

This is a very powerful briefing move. It gives the player a reason to behave in ways that are intelligible from the outside but not fully predictable. Publicly they may appear status quo. Privately they may welcome transformation. That gap is where negotiation, deception, and strategic ambiguity live.

## Briefs should read like intelligence, not forms

The handbook warns against overusing analytical frameworks like DIME and PMESII in the written brief itself. As design checklists they can be helpful for surfacing categories of leverage the designer might otherwise miss, but if they are translated directly into the player-facing brief, the result tends to become sterile and over-systematized. The system reference implicitly agrees by presenting briefs in prose bullets rather than in ratings or taxonomies.

This is a useful warning because designers often confuse clarity with tabulation. A line like “informationally strong through treaty archives and unpublished clauses” is much more playable than a quadrant marked “Informational: High”. The former suggests actions and constraints. The latter only classifies.

So a strong brief should read like concise intelligence. It should be legible, concrete, and strategic, but still written as sentences about a world rather than as a spreadsheet about a unit. That style is not cosmetic. It directly supports better leverage arguments later.

## The general brief matters too

When people talk about briefs, they sometimes mean only the private faction sheets. But the system reference is clear that there is also a general brief distributed to everyone, containing the problem, broad world context, one public sentence per faction, the action structure, the game’s fixed endpoint, and the expectations formula that the goal is to achieve objectives while the point is to create a credible narrative. The handbook includes the same elements in its blank template and worked material.

This general brief does important work. It ensures that players share a common frame before asymmetry begins to operate. Without it, the referee ends up patching foundational misunderstandings through ad hoc clarification, which the later Turn Zero material is partly designed to prevent. Private asymmetry works best when public foundations are stable.

So writing good briefs is not only about each faction sheet. It is also about making sure the common briefing gives everyone the same understanding of the problem, the turn loop, and the criteria of play.

## A practical brief checklist

A brief is probably ready when you can answer yes to most of these.

- Does it clearly state two paired objectives, one short-term and one long-term?
- Do those objectives pass the pithy test and sound like something the faction would actually fight for?
- Does the position field describe only what is especially significant relative to other factions?
- Are the position bullets specific enough to support concrete leverage arguments?
- Is there a meaningful public/private split where it helps create tension?
- Have you removed background material that would not affect decisions at the table?

If not, the brief may still be informative, but it probably is not yet operational.

## Why briefs are where design becomes playable

The handbook says designers often skip too quickly from problem to briefs without fully resolving Actors first, which produces briefs whose objectives and positions could belong to almost anyone. The system reference encodes the same lesson by putting Actor design before brief writing in the workflow. That sequence matters because a brief is only as good as the strategic identity it expresses.

But once the faction is well designed, the brief is where that design becomes real. It is where identity turns into direction, leverage, and constraint. Players do not act out of abstract design principles. They act out of what the brief tells them they need, fear, hold, and can plausibly attempt.
