# How to Build Good OSG Factions

If the problem statement creates the field of conflict, factions determine whether that field becomes interesting. A weak faction roster can flatten even a good scenario into repetitive play, while a strong roster can make the same crisis feel contested from multiple incompatible angles. The handbook and system reference are unusually clear on this point: factions should differ by leverage type rather than simple power level, and a faction is not really designed until you can say what it is, what it wants, and what makes it unlike any other faction in the game.

That gives a good starting principle for this post. Faction design in OSG is not about producing exhaustive lore profiles. It is about giving each player a strategic identity clear enough to generate distinctive action choices under pressure. If two factions would approach the problem in basically the same way, one of them is probably redundant.

## Start with three bullets

The handbook gives a very disciplined test for faction design: before writing a full brief, draft three bullets that capture what the faction fundamentally is, what it wants and why, and what makes it unlike any other faction in this specific game. The system reference repeats the same structure almost verbatim and says that if you cannot write those bullets distinctly, the faction is not yet designed.

This is a useful corrective because it pushes against overdesign. Many designers are tempted to build faction histories, ideologies, symbols, and internal substructures before they have identified the faction’s actual function in play. The OSG texts treat that as backward. The first task is not comprehensive profile-building but isolating the few facts a player needs to make interesting decisions immediately.

The Essaveth example in the handbook shows how compressed this can be. Essaveth sits at a geographic crossroads, its borders depend on the old treaty order, and its garrison can hold for weeks but not months; from those three points, a player can already see the faction’s leverage, vulnerability, and urgency. That is a good standard: three sentences should already imply a strategic posture.

## Different leverage, not different sizes

The core design principle is leverage asymmetry. The handbook says factions should differ by the type of leverage they hold, not by raw power level, because “power parity” where everyone tries to do the same things with more or less strength produces dull play. The system reference makes the same point and lists four primary leverage types as recurring design tools: military, economic, geographic, and informational.

These categories matter because they produce different action logics. A military faction pushes through force or threat, but pays costs when it acts openly. An economic faction shapes what others can sustain over time through dependency, finance, and market control. A geographic faction controls movement, access, chokepoints, or adjacency. An informational faction creates surprise, uncertainty, or institutional advantage through secrets, communications, records, or narrative control.

Good faction design usually gives each faction a clear primary leverage type and a secondary one that creates tradeoffs rather than solving every problem. Caldrath in the handbook’s worked example has military strength but legitimacy problems and dependence on others; Meranto has leverage over everyone’s finances but cannot apply it without risk to itself; Saivorn controls ports and trade but lacks a standing army. Those asymmetries are what make alliances necessary rather than ornamental.

## Specificity is what makes leverage real

The handbook’s specificity principle is one of the best tools in the whole corpus. “Has resources” is not a useful position statement; “controls the only two deep-water ports on the peninsula” is. The system reference uses almost the same contrast and says a position bullet is too vague if two readers could interpret it in materially different ways and build different leverage arguments from it.

This matters because vague positions produce vague actions. If a brief tells a player only that they are wealthy, influential, or well connected, the player cannot tell what counts as credible leverage and the referee cannot adjudicate consistently. Specificity gives both sides something to point at.

The practical test is simple. Every important position bullet should tell the player what this faction has, lacks, or faces that would surprise someone who only knew the general situation. A good bullet is not generic worldbuilding; it is usable strategic information.

## Objectives should create tension, not paperwork

Once the faction identity is clear, the brief gives it direction. The handbook and system reference both say that every brief has two mandatory fields: objectives and position. Objectives should come in a short-term and long-term pair, with the short-term being achievable within the game’s turn count and the long-term representing a broader condition that may only be partially realized by the end.

This pairing does several things at once. The short-term objective prevents early drift by giving the player something concrete to pursue in the first turns. The long-term objective stops the game from collapsing into local opportunism by making the final state of the world matter, not just the last tactical exchange. Most importantly, the two should be capable of conflict: the move that secures immediate advantage may damage the conditions needed for long-term success.

The Essaveth example in the handbook makes this very clear. Securing outside guarantees quickly may require compromises that weaken later treaty protection, while holding out for a better final settlement risks losing immediate security before the army moves. That is what good objectives do: they generate strategic tension inside the faction, not just between factions.

The handbook also offers a useful style test: add an exclamation mark and read the objective aloud. If it sounds like something a faction leader would actually say with conviction, it probably works. If it sounds like a policy memo, it probably needs revision.

## The brief is operational, not literary

A strong faction brief is not there to immerse the player in backstory for its own sake. The handbook says the brief is not background reading but operational, and that everything in it should be something the Actor will use. The system reference agrees and defines the position field not as an inventory, but as what is especially significant about this faction relative to others.

That distinction keeps briefs lean and effective. Include specific assets, vulnerabilities, key relationships, and geographic or positional constraints that affect action design. Exclude material that is true of everyone, lore that will not affect decisions, and private information the faction has no way to use. A good brief tells the player where their pressure points are.

The Conclave example in the handbook is especially good here. It holds the original treaty texts, occupies an extraterritorial temple complex, and has no military while carrying debt to Meranto. Each point is both flavorful and immediately actionable: information can be revealed or withheld, sacred space offers protection at political cost rather than immunity, and debt constrains the faction’s freedom. That is what “operational” looks like.

## Avoid the three flat archetypes

The handbook identifies three recurring faction-design failures: the pure military faction, the pure diplomat, and the pure wildcard. The system reference repeats the same traps in condensed form. These are valuable warnings because each one fails in a different way.

The pure military faction is flat because every problem has the same answer: apply force. Without a serious non-military vulnerability, the player is pushed toward repetitive decisions and other forms of leverage start to feel irrelevant. The pure diplomat is flat because it may have no real stakes or exposure; if it can never be directly threatened, its choices may become costless. The pure wildcard is flat because incoherent objectives do not produce meaningful arcs, and a player with no clear opposed interests has little basis for self-assessment at the end.

The fixes are structural rather than cosmetic. Give the military faction dependence or fragility outside its main strength. Give the diplomat goals that cannot be secured by talking alone and at least one hostile relationship. Give the wildcard real conflicts with at least two other factions so unpredictability emerges from pressure rather than from emptiness.

## Factions must conflict in more than one direction

A faction is not interesting only because it has a clear identity. It is interesting because that identity intersects the problem and the other factions in ways that create pressure from several angles. The handbook’s pre-game checklist asks whether every faction has objectives that conflict with at least two others, and the system reference does the same. That is a strong design rule.

Why at least two? Because one opponent creates a duel, not a political field. A faction with only one meaningful antagonist tends to lock the game into stable blocs very quickly. A better faction can be aligned with one player on one front, opposed on another, and opportunistically useful to a third for reasons none of them fully control.

The Corentine example is helpful because none of the five factions are redundant, but none are isolated either. Caldrath needs Essaveth geographically, needs legitimacy from others, and may be constrained by Meranto financially. Saivorn wants open trade but does not necessarily want the old order restored. The Conclave can deny recognition without being able to enforce outcomes alone. This is the pattern to aim for: multi-directional entanglement.

## Player count shapes faction design

Faction design is also affected by roster size. Both the handbook and the system reference treat five to eight Actors as the sweet spot, because fewer than five tends to collapse alliance dynamics and more than eight creates report overload and cognitive strain. That means faction design should not be thought about independently from expected player count.

With fewer factions, each one must carry more weight and often needs built-in tension within likely coalitions so the game does not settle too quickly. With more factions, differentiation becomes harder, because too many nearby positions blur into one another. This is another reason leverage type matters so much. As numbers rise, “same but stronger” design becomes increasingly unreadable.

The sources therefore recommend using overflow roles rather than simply adding more full factions once the count gets too high. That recommendation is not only about logistics. It also protects faction identity from dilution.

## A practical faction checklist

A faction is probably ready when you can answer yes to most of the following.

- Can you state what it is, what it wants, and what makes it unlike others in three bullets?
- Does it have a clear primary leverage type and a secondary one that creates tradeoffs?
- Are its position bullets specific enough to support concrete leverage arguments?
- Do its objectives form a short-term and long-term pair with real tension between them?
- Do those objectives conflict with at least two other factions?
- Have you avoided the pure-military, pure-diplomat, and pure-wildcard traps?

If not, the faction probably needs revision before the briefing stage.

## Why faction design sits at the center

The handbook says designers most often skip from problem straight to briefs without fully resolving Actors first, and that this creates factions whose objectives could belong to anyone. The system reference encodes the same logic by placing Actors before Briefs in the design order and treating Actor differentiation as a prerequisite for meaningful objectives. That sequencing matters.

In practice, faction design is where a problem statement becomes playable. The crisis may be strong, but until it is refracted through distinct strategic identities, players are not really making different choices for different reasons. Good factions turn a scenario from a topic into a contest.
