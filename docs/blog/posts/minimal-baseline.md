# Minimal Baseline: What an OSG Must Have to Be Recognizably OSG

Once a term starts to become useful, the next argument is unavoidable: useful for what, exactly? If “Open Strategy Game” names a recognizable form, then there must be some line between a game that is recognizably OSG and a game that merely resembles it in passing. The hard part is drawing that line without pretending it is eternal, official, or beyond dispute. The source texts themselves advise against that kind of false finality. The guiding-principles document explicitly says it is “NOT a definition” and that OSGs may deviate, while the handbook frames OSG as a playable design framework rather than a rigid definition.

So this article is not proposing a creed. It is proposing a baseline: the smallest cluster of features that needs to be present before the form is still recognizably OSG rather than simply adjacent to it. That baseline should be strict enough to be useful and modest enough to leave room for real variation.

My claim is simple. A recognizably OSG needs at least these things: players act as factions rather than single protagonists, the game centers on a shared crisis or problem, each faction gets one action per turn, actions are expressed through action/outcome/leverage, actions are submitted privately to a referee, the referee adjudicates outcomes against the established fiction, a public report resets the strategic situation each turn, the game ends after a fixed number of turns, and play concludes with self-assessment and debrief. Remove enough of those elements, and the form stops being clearly OSG.

## Start with the role

The first non-negotiable is that the player role is factional. The handbook and system reference are both explicit on this point: in OSG, you play a faction, not a character, not a hero, and not a single protagonist moving through someone else’s narrative. That distinction is not cosmetic. It changes what decisions mean.

A faction acts at a different scale from a character. It has interests, leverage, constraints, and objectives that may involve institutions, armies, ports, debts, trade routes, legitimacy, or survival across a whole region or crisis. Once you lose that factional role and reduce the player back to an individual protagonist, the game may still be strategic, political, or open-ended, but it is no longer recognizably the same form.

This is also why “multiple roles in a consistent world” appears so early in the guiding principles. The player is not just inhabiting a perspective. They are occupying a strategic position relative to other positions, and that relational structure is part of the baseline.

## The game needs a shared problem

The second baseline feature is a common crisis that every faction must respond to, though not in the same way. The handbook describes OSG’s common structure as beginning from a shared problem, and the system reference defines the game as a shared crisis or structural problem that every faction must respond to, but that none will solve in the same way. This is one of the clearest points of continuity across the texts.

Why does this matter so much? Because without a shared problem, the game disperses into parallel solo campaigns. Factions may still exist, and they may even interfere with one another occasionally, but the pressure that makes OSG feel like a single contested situation is gone. The shared problem is what creates the field of collision.

The design sections make this concrete. The problem statement is not a setting overview, not a list of objectives, and not a question. It is the disturbance that generates multiple plausible responses without dictating one correct answer. If a game lacks that common pressure point, it may still be a scenario game or a political roleplay exercise, but it is no longer centered in the same way.

## One action per turn

The single-action rule belongs in the baseline. Both the handbook and the system reference state it directly: each Actor submits exactly one action per turn. This is not a convenience rule. It shapes the entire strategic economy of play.

The handbook explains why in practical and structural terms. One action forces prioritization, creates vulnerability, prevents everyone from pushing on every front at once, and keeps the referee’s adjudication and reporting load coherent. The system reference says the same rule is part of the game’s core turn structure, and that bundling two actions together is something the referee should reject and return for revision.

Could a variant break this rule and still be OSG-adjacent? Possibly. The handbook even notes variants with a leader action or secondary action, but it presents these as variants that increase complexity rather than as part of the default form. That is exactly why the single-action rule belongs in the baseline. It is the center from which the variants depart.

## Action, outcome, leverage

A recognizably OSG also needs the three-part action structure. Across the texts, players do not merely announce an intention. They state what they are doing, what result they want, and why that result is plausible given their position and the established fiction. In the later vocabulary, those parts become Action, Outcome, and Leverage.

This matters because the three parts do distinct jobs. Action forces commitment to a specific course of conduct. Outcome separates means from desired result, so the referee is adjudicating what happens rather than rubber-stamping the player’s desired effect. Leverage requires the player to ground the attempt in resources, relationships, geography, prior actions, or other established facts rather than in wishful thinking. Remove that structure, and adjudication becomes vaguer, less traceable, and more arbitrary.

The guiding principles present the same core pattern in plainer terms: players tell the referee one specific action, including why it is likely to succeed and what intended result they are aiming for. That is enough continuity to treat the three-part structure as baseline rather than optional style.

## Private submission and referee adjudication

The next baseline element is harder to separate into pieces because the texts consistently treat them as linked: players submit actions privately, and the referee adjudicates them against the established fiction. This is one of the strongest markers of recognizability in the form.

Private submission matters because it prevents reactive optimization from swallowing strategy. The handbook says that if players see one another’s actions before committing to their own, they will tend to counter the most threatening move rather than pursue their own agenda, and covert operations lose their force if everything is revealed in advance. The system reference repeats the same logic and ties private submission directly to the avoidance of reactive play.

Referee adjudication matters for a different reason. OSG is not built around exhaustive resolution procedures for every possible domain of action. The referee judges whether leverage is strong or weak relative to opposition, applies the resolution system, and writes outcomes consistent with the fiction. The referee is not authoring a plot, but neither are they absent. Their role is structurally central.

This is also the point where OSG draws one of its clearest lines against more public, debate-heavy forms of matrix play. The handbook explicitly contrasts public argument with what it calls “benevolent dictatorship,” meaning one referee, full authority, no public argument before the ruling. The system reference presents the same model as standard. Since the form is being named partly to describe that entertainment-play adaptation, private submission plus referee adjudication belongs in the baseline.

## Public reports are part of the engine

A surprising number of people would probably omit the report if asked to sketch the core of OSG. That would be a mistake. The report is not housekeeping. It is one of the central mechanisms by which the game becomes legible to itself.

The handbook calls the report the “heartbeat” of the game and describes the turn cycle as a movement from deadline, to dark period, to public revelation, to renewed action. The system reference says the report does two things at once: it chronicles what happened and reseeds the game for the next turn. This is more than mere recap. It is what turns private adjudications into a shared strategic situation.

That is why a baseline OSG needs public reports, not just private rulings. Without the report, the game fragments into hidden one-to-one exchanges with the referee. Players lose the common public world they are supposed to be responding to. Hooks disappear, traceability weakens, and the shared crisis becomes harder to perceive turn by turn. A report can vary in style and length, but some recognizable public reset mechanism belongs in the baseline.

## Fixed end and debrief

The final two baseline features are closure conditions: the game ends after a set number of turns, and it concludes with self-assessment and debrief. These are easy to underestimate because they happen at the end, but the sources treat them as structurally important from the beginning.

A fixed endpoint matters because it shapes decision-making all game long. If players know the game lasts six turns, then timing, sacrifice, short-term positioning, and long-term groundwork all take on a specific strategic meaning. Without that boundary, the form starts drifting toward open campaign play, where decision-making can defer consequences indefinitely.

The debrief matters because OSG does not end when the last action is resolved. The handbook is emphatic here: self-assessment is built into the structure, but without a shared conversation it remains private and incomplete; the debrief is where players reveal objectives, explain what they think happened, contest one another’s interpretations, and reflect on what the scenario actually produced. The system reference says the same thing more bluntly: “Run the debrief. Always”.

This is a strong signal that the debrief belongs in the baseline, not in the “nice if there’s time” category. A game that drops the debrief may still borrow OSG-like procedures during play, but it has removed one of the form’s defining closure mechanisms.

## What is not baseline

Once the baseline is stated, it becomes easier to say what does not need to be present. The sources are especially useful here because they explicitly separate the design minimum from optional or strongly recommended additions.

A formal map is strongly recommended, but not strictly required. The handbook says “Problem, Actors, Briefs is enough to run a game,” and that the map, while strongly recommended, is not mandatory for play to happen. The system reference says the same in slightly different terms by naming Problem, Actors, and Briefs as the design minimum, with map strongly recommended and bonuses and NPAs optional.

Spendable bonuses are optional. NPAs are optional. Overflow-player roles such as Consultant or Team Actor are participation structures for scaling, not baseline identity. Turn Zero is valuable and often important, but the handbook explicitly allows that it can be abbreviated for experienced groups. Even the exact resolution variant can move somewhat at the edges, as shown by the BLOC variant, though the baseline texts clearly treat the keep-high/keep-low model as standard.

This negative definition matters because it prevents baseline from turning into bloat. A baseline that includes every recommended design improvement is no longer a baseline.

## A practical baseline test

If you want a compact recognition test, ask these questions.

- Are players acting as factions, not single protagonists?
- Is there a shared crisis or structural problem that all factions must respond to?
- Does each faction get one action per turn?
- Is the action structured around what is being done, what outcome is sought, and what leverage supports it?
- Are actions submitted privately to a referee rather than openly sequenced for reaction?
- Does the referee adjudicate according to the established fiction rather than rely on exhaustive subsystems ?
- Is there a public report that turns outcomes into a new shared situation?
- Does the game end after a fixed number of turns and conclude with self-assessment plus debrief?

If the answer to several of those is no, the game may still be interesting, but the burden of proof that it is recognizably OSG gets much heavier.

## Why keep the baseline minimal

There is a final reason to keep this baseline modest. The guiding-principles text warns against treating any current formulation as the final definition, and the handbook repeatedly presents OSG as a framework that can vary in genre, scale, and texture. A baseline should therefore do one thing well: identify the center clearly enough that meaningful variation remains possible around it.

That is why this baseline focuses on roles, turn structure, adjudication logic, reporting, and closure. Those are the things most deeply tied to the form’s identity across the texts. They are not the only good ideas in OSG practice, but they are the cluster most necessary for recognition.

Once that center is clear, the next question becomes easier and more interesting: what can change without breaking the form? Which variations are genuine variation, and which ones cross the line into a different kind of game altogether? That is the next post.
