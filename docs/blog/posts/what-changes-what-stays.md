# What Changes, What Stays: Variation in OSG

Once you sketch a minimal baseline for OSG, two bad habits become tempting. One is to treat every common practice as mandatory. The other is to treat the term as so open that almost any adjacent game can fit inside it. The source texts push against both errors. They describe OSG as a recognizable framework with a stable core, but they also repeatedly stress that scenarios can vary widely in genre, scale, texture, and implementation, and that the guiding principles are not a final definition. So the useful question is not whether variation exists. It obviously does. The useful question is which kinds of variation leave the form intact.

The answer, in broad terms, is this: a lot can vary as long as the central loop still survives. If you still have faction play, a shared problem, one action per turn, action/outcome/leverage, referee adjudication, public reports, a fixed endpoint, and debrief, you have room to change a surprising amount around the edges. Genre can change. Scale can change. Maps can shrink or disappear. Optional subsystems can come and go. Participation structures can be redesigned. Even some adjudication variants can be introduced. What breaks the form is not variation itself, but variation that replaces the core loop with a different one.

## Genre can vary a lot

Genre is probably the easiest thing to vary, and the handbook is explicit that OSG scenarios can differ widely in genre, scale, and texture. The later sections push this further by describing how the same core adjudication logic carries across war, politics, mythology, ecology, corporate conflict, and supernatural play, while changing what kinds of leverage arguments dominate and what sorts of Forces of Nature make sense in the fiction.

That is a strong sign that genre is not part of the baseline identity. In a war scenario, strong leverage may come from force projection, logistics, or territorial control. In a political scenario, the same structural role may be played by information, legitimacy, relationships, or procedural authority. In mythology, precedent, divine mandate, or symbolic resonance can become credible leverage, while in corporate play the key pressures may be financial position, intelligence asymmetry, and regulatory capture. The fictional vocabulary changes, but the core activity does not.

This matters because some forms are tied tightly to one genre even when they are technically adaptable. OSG does not seem to be one of them. The texts describe it as argument-based and therefore portable across setting types. That portability is not a side effect. It is part of what the term is for.

## Scale can change too

OSG is also flexible in scale, though not without practical consequences. The handbook describes the form as working across a significant range of player counts and notes that the dynamics shift with scale rather than the identity of the form vanishing immediately. The system reference also assumes a range of five to eight Actors as the sweet spot, but presents that as an optimal band for alliance dynamics and referee workload, not as a hard ontological boundary.

With three to five Actors, the game becomes tighter and more overdetermined, because each faction matters more and alliances form quickly. With five to eight, the system hits its preferred density of shifting coalitions and readable reports. Beyond that, the workload and cognitive load rise sharply, but the recommended solution is not “stop calling it OSG.” It is to use Extras systems, Team Actors, Consultants, or other participation structures that preserve the core loop without multiplying adjudicated Actors indefinitely.

So scale can vary, but scale pressures design. That distinction is useful. Something can remain recognizably OSG even if it is running small or large, provided it protects the same turn economy and strategic interaction. What changes is not the form’s identity but the referee’s burden and the structure needed to support it.

## The map can vary, and can even be minimal

One of the clearest examples of “strongly recommended but not mandatory” is the map. The handbook says directly that “Problem, Actors, Briefs is enough to run a game,” while also insisting that the map is strongly recommended. The system reference says the same thing in its design minimum table: Problem, Actors, and Briefs are the minimum; map is strongly recommended; bonuses and NPAs are optional.

This means a map is not part of the absolute baseline, but map design is still central to many good OSGs. The texts emphasize that maps provide an argumentative surface, make contested spaces legible, and give leverage arguments about movement, access, terrain, and control something concrete to refer to. They also allow physical components and tokens to make the world state readable at a glance.

But the same texts leave room for simpler implementations. A schematic diagram can replace a geographic map. A handmade sketch can replace a polished one. A verbal geography with a few named locations can work in a pinch. The key question is not whether the map is elaborate. It is whether the world state remains arguable and legible. That is why map format can vary without breaking the form.

## Bonuses are optional

Spendable bonuses are a good example of something common enough to be worth discussing in depth, but not central enough to define the form. The handbook says first-time referees should design without them and add them later if needed, and the system reference lists them as optional. That is a strong signal that bonuses belong outside the baseline.

When used, bonuses create another layer of asymmetry. They give factions one-use or limited-use assets tied to starting position, which can shift leverage in specific contexts and create timing decisions about deployment. The sources are careful to say that a bonus should shift leverage, not guarantee success, and that bonuses should be narratively named and contextually bounded. In other words, they are a useful design tool, not the engine itself.

This is exactly the kind of variation a flexible form should allow. An OSG with bonuses and one without bonuses can still be recognizably the same form if the core turn structure and adjudication logic remain intact. The presence or absence of bonuses changes strategic texture, not basic identity.

## NPAs can be added or omitted

Non-Player Actors are another optional layer. The system reference defines them clearly and explains when to use them, but also says not to use them when player pressure is already sufficient, when they would crowd out player agency, or when the referee is already at capacity. The handbook makes the same point by listing NPAs after the core design steps and treating them as one way to add world pressure, not as something every scenario requires.

This is important because NPAs can make an OSG feel more alive, but they also create risks. Since the referee controls them while also adjudicating everyone else, bias and overload become real concerns. The texts therefore discuss mitigations, simplified NPA briefs, and random-control options for chaotic entities. All of that only makes sense if NPAs are optional modules rather than identity markers.

So an OSG can have no NPAs, a few restrained NPAs, or specially structured NPAs, and still remain recognizably itself. What matters is whether they support the core faction-driven contest rather than displace it.

## Participation structures can vary

The standard assumption is one player per faction, but the texts leave clear room for other participation structures. The system reference includes portable roles such as Consultant and Team Actor, and the handbook expands this into scenario-specific structures where overflow players may act as an electorate, a mob, or other fiction-shaped collectives. These are treated as established practices, but not as mandatory ones.

That distinction is useful. If you have eight players and six factions, splitting one faction into a Team Actor or giving extra players Consultant roles does not change the game into something else. Likewise, if the fiction supports it, building a voting public or chaotic crowd into the scenario can still fit OSG so long as the participation structure remains legible and tied to the fiction. What changes is how input enters the system, not the existence of the system itself.

This flexibility matters because it shows that “one human equals one faction” is not part of the essence. What matters is that the game still revolves around factions confronting a shared problem through adjudicated strategic action. Participation can be distributed in different ways.

## Timing can change

The turn window is another variable. The handbook and system reference both describe asynchronous play with something like a 48-hour action window as a practical default, but they also state that live play is possible and that the same discipline can be compressed from days to minutes. This is an important point because it shows that form is not dependent on a particular pacing medium.

An async OSG tends to produce more negotiation between reports, more reflective action drafting, and greater emphasis on written communication. A live OSG compresses those processes, making them more immediate and perhaps more socially intense. But if the turn still consists of action submission, adjudication, report, and renewed action, then the identity remains recognizable.

This is another case where variation changes texture more than form. A different tempo can produce a different feel, but tempo alone does not turn an OSG into a different kind of game.

## Some adjudication variants can survive

Adjudication is closer to the core, so variation here is trickier. Still, the handbook provides evidence that not every departure from the default break the form. The BLOC variant, for example, introduces crowdsourced disadvantages, a dice-pool style resolution in place of the simple 2d6 keep-high or keep-low method, and a six-result table rather than the more compressed outcome bands.

What is important is how the handbook frames this. It calls BLOC a variant that differs from the baseline in significant ways, especially by increasing player involvement in challenging actions and slowing the turn cycle. But it does not treat BLOC as having become non-OSG merely by adding granularity. The reason appears to be that the deeper structure still survives: factions still submit actions, leverage still matters, adjudication still interprets attempts against fiction, and the report cycle still drives play.

This suggests a useful rule of thumb. Adjudication can vary in instrumentation as long as it preserves the same underlying logic: argument-based action, qualitative leverage assessment, variable outcome, and consequences that reshape the shared world. What would break the form is replacing that logic with something fundamentally different, such as a closed tactical subsystem that resolves most meaningful play independently of referee judgment.

## Texture can vary, but not direction

The handbook’s phrase “genre, scale, and texture” is especially useful here. Texture includes many small decisions that are easy to overstate. Reports can be terse or a little more atmospheric, as long as they remain factual enough to seed new hooks and do not become buried in narrative prose. Closing frames before debrief can be tables or short epilogues. Briefs can be denser or leaner, public and private information can be split or integrated differently, and the social culture at the table can be more formal or more relaxed.

These variations matter in play, but they do not necessarily alter the form’s direction. That is the key distinction. A change in texture changes how the form feels. A change in direction changes what the form is trying to do.

The sources help here because they keep restating the direction in simple terms: the goal is to achieve your objectives; the point is to create a credible narrative. As long as a variation still serves that dual aim through factional action and adjudicated consequence, texture can move quite a lot without loss of identity.

## What probably does break the form

Variation has limits. The easiest way to see those limits is by contrast. If you remove private submission and make play primarily reactive to public declarations, you are moving away from the OSG turn economy described in the texts. If you replace referee adjudication with exhaustive subsystems for every important domain, you are moving back toward a more closed wargame logic. If you abandon faction play in favor of single protagonists, you drift toward a different family of RPG play. If you remove reports, fixed endpoint, or debrief, you weaken the loop that gives OSG its public rhythm and reflective closure.

That is why the right question is not “can this vary?” but “does this variation preserve the core loop?” The form breaks when the answer becomes no.

## A practical test for variation

A good way to evaluate a variant is to ask four questions.

- Does this change preserve faction-based strategic play around a shared problem?
- Does it preserve the one-action turn economy and the action/outcome/leverage logic, even if phrased differently?
- Does it preserve referee-led adjudication and public reports as the main engine of world change?
- Does it preserve fixed-end self-assessment and debrief as part of the game’s closure?

If yes, there is a good chance the change is variation rather than replacement. If no, then the game may still be worthwhile, but it is probably moving into another form.

## Why this flexibility matters

This flexibility is not an accident. The handbook explicitly presents OSG as a design framework rather than a rigid definition, and the system reference is written as a generic, setting-neutral framework that can be adapted and built on. The guiding-principles text reinforces the point by warning that it is not a definition and that OSGs may deviate. Taken together, those sources suggest that the form was named not to freeze practice, but to make variation discussable without losing sight of the center.

That is why it is worth being precise about what can change. A form that cannot vary tends to calcify into product identity. A form that cannot say what varies meaningfully tends to dissolve into vagueness. OSG sits between those failures. It has a center, and it has room.
